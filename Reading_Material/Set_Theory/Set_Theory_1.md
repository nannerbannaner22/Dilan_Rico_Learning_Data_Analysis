## **CHAPTER ONE: THE HORROR OF THINGS**

Look around you.

Go on. Have a good stare.

What can you see?

A chair? A sock? A biscuit crumb that has somehow survived three hoovers, two mops and one very suspicious dog? Congratulations — you are already surrounded by **things**.

And mathematicians LOVE things.

That may sound disappointing. You were probably hoping mathematicians spend their time leaping across volcanoes, wrestling panthers and shouting, *“Quick! Hand me the emergency calculator!”*

They do not.

Mostly, they stare at collections of stuff and say things like, *“Hmm. Interesting. These objects appear to belong together.”*

Which is exactly why **set theory** was invented.

## **WHAT IS A SET?**

A **set** is just a collection of things.

That’s it.

Really.

A set can be:

- a set of spoons,
- a set of worms,
- a set of numbers,
- a set of your friend's disgusting habits,
- a set of people who say *“To be honest...”* just before saying something rude.

If it can be collected together and thought about as a single bunch, it can be a set.

Easy.

Or is it?

Because the moment mathematicians got hold of sets, they didn’t stop at *“a bunch of stuff.”*

Oh no.

They started asking terrifying questions.

- Which things belong in the set?
- Which things do not?
- Can a thing belong to more than one set?
- Can a set contain nothing at all?
- Can a set contain... other sets?

At this point, normal people sensibly back away and go eat toast.

Mathematicians lean forward with glittering eyes.

## **THE SOCK DISASTER**

Imagine a set of all your socks.

That seems harmless enough, unless your socks are particularly sinister.

Now imagine:

- the set of all the odd socks,
- the set of all the socks with holes in,
- the set of all the socks that are odd **and** holey.

This is the sort of thing set theory does. It takes a perfectly normal pile of laundry and turns it into a brain puzzle.

And laundry is only the beginning.

Set theory is one of the great secret machines of mathematics. It helps sort numbers, explain logic, organise ideas and build vast towers of maths out of almost nothing.

It is tidy.

It is powerful.

And, in the wrong hands, it is deeply annoying.

## **THE EMPTY SET: A BOX OF NOTHING**

Because once someone starts talking in sets, they can say outrageous things with a completely straight face.

For example:

> *“There is a set with nothing in it.”*

How can that be a set?

If there’s nothing in it, surely it’s just... nothing?

Wrong!

It’s the **empty set**.

Mathematicians were so pleased with this idea that they gave it its own symbol:

$$
\varnothing
$$

Which looks a bit like a zero that has had a terrible accident.

---

> [!WARNING]
> ### **DO NOT PANIC**
> The empty set is still a set.
>
> It just has no members.
>
> Think of it as a party to which absolutely nobody came.

## **SYMBOLS OF DOOM**

Soon you’ll meet **unions**, **intersections**, **subsets** and diagrams full of overlapping blobs. You’ll see numbers sorted into gangs, ideas trapped inside circles, and strange symbols marching across the page as if they own the place.

Here are a few of the most important ones.

---

### **1. Membership**
If something belongs to a set, mathematicians write:

$$
x \in A
$$

That means:

> \(x\) is in set \(A\)

If it does **not** belong, they write:

$$
x \notin A
$$

---

### **2. Empty Set**
The set with nothing in it:

$$
\varnothing
$$

Tiny. Empty. Suspicious.

---

### **3. Subset**
If every element of set \(A\) is also in set \(B\), then:

$$
A \subseteq B
$$

That means \(A\) fits entirely inside \(B\), like a smaller disgusting lunchbox inside a larger disgusting lunchbox.

---

### **4. Union**
If you mash two sets together into one big set:

$$
A \cup B
$$

That is called the **union**.

It means: everything in \(A\), everything in \(B\), and no complaining.

---

### **5. Intersection**
If you only want the things the two sets have in common:

$$
A \cap B
$$

That is called the **intersection**.

This is the mathematical version of asking:

> “What do these two horrible groups both contain?”

---

### **6. Curly Brackets**
Sets are usually written with curly brackets like this:

$$
\{1,2,3\}
$$

or

$$
\{\text{cat}, \text{sock}, \text{worm}\}
$$

Curly brackets are like little fences for trapping things.

## **MINI SIDEBAR: SETS IN THE WILD**

You may think sets are just silly maths blobs.

Sets are used in:

## **THE BLOBBY DIAGRAMS**

Sometimes mathematicians draw sets as circles or blobs.

These are called **Venn diagrams**.

One blob might be:

- all hairy things,

and another blob might be:

- all things that smell suspicious.

Where the blobs overlap, you get the things that are both hairy **and** suspicious.

Which, sadly, includes more real-life objects than anyone would admit.

---

> [!TIP]
> ### **BLOB RULE**
> In a Venn diagram:
> - the whole blob is the set,
> - anything inside belongs,
> - anything outside does not.
>
> So if a worm is inside the “Disgusting Things” blob, mathematics has spoken.

## **A PROPERLY HORRIBLE EXAMPLE**

Let

$$
A = \{\text{slug}, \text{sock}, \text{toothbrush}\}
$$

and

$$
B = \{\text{sock}, \text{worm}, \text{banana peel}\}
$$

Then:

### **Union**
Everything in either set:

$$
A \cup B = \{\text{slug}, \text{sock}, \text{toothbrush}, \text{worm}, \text{banana peel}\}
$$

### **Intersection**
Only the things they have in common:

$$
A \cap B = \{\text{sock}\}
$$

So the sock is in both sets.

Which suggests you should probably throw it away.
